Automotive · Fabrication

Mazda 3 Front-End Rebuild

Diagnosed and repaired a post-collision engine non-start using custom vacuum line engineering — no replacement parts, no guesswork.

The Problem

After a front-end collision, the vehicle exhibited a hard non-start and severe rough idle. Standard diagnostics pointed to vacuum system faults, but the root cause wasn't immediately obvious — damage was subtle and the failure mode wasn't well-documented for this platform.

Root Cause Analysis

Systematic inspection of the intake manifold revealed fractured vacuum nipples — small, easy to overlook, but catastrophic for air-fuel metering. The cracks were allowing unmetered air into the intake, leaning out the mixture and preventing reliable combustion.

Engineering Solution

Rather than sourcing a new manifold, a custom bypass solution was engineered: the damaged ports were blocked and sealed, new ports were precision-tapped at correct geometry, and custom brass fittings with new vacuum lines were installed. Each junction was leak-tested before reinstallation.

Result

Vehicle returned to full operation. Proper air-fuel behavior restored. The vehicle has run reliably for months post-repair with zero recurrence — validating the custom solution over an OEM replacement approach.

Machine Design · Analysis

Single-Stage Spur Gearbox

Designed a 4 hp gearbox with 4.52:1 reduction using full AGMA methodology — through repeated failure, redesign, and validation.

Design Specifications

Design target: transmit 4 hp at 2000 rpm input with a 4.52:1 gear ratio. Constraints included manufacturability, cost, and long-term fatigue reliability. No single design iteration satisfied all constraints simultaneously — the process demanded trade-off analysis at every stage.

Analysis Methodology

Applied full AGMA stress analysis across multiple gear geometries, evaluating both bending fatigue and contact stress. Fatigue calculations used safety factors calibrated to realistic duty cycles. Multiple designs were analytically "failed" before a geometry meeting all criteria was reached.

Iteration Process

Early designs failed on contact stress margins. Mid-iteration designs resolved stress but over-engineered the face width, inflating cost. Final design balanced gear module, tooth count, and face width to satisfy AGMA minimums while remaining manufacturable on standard equipment.

Outcome

Final gearbox design met all stress, fatigue, and manufacturability criteria. The iterative process — analytically failing designs early — is the core lesson: good engineering is not finding the first solution that works, but finding the one that works best under all constraints.

FEA · Thermal Simulation

Brake Rotor Thermal Analysis

Simulated transient braking loads on a 1400 kg vehicle and optimized rotor geometry to reduce peak temperatures by ~8%.

Simulation Setup

Modeled a solid brake rotor and subjected it to a transient thermal analysis simulating emergency braking: 1400 kg vehicle decelerating from 27 m/s (≈97 km/h) to rest over 3 seconds. Heat generation was calculated from kinetic energy dissipation and applied as a time-varying surface heat flux.

Thermal Loading

Boundary conditions included convective cooling from rotor surfaces (accounting for rotational speed-dependent convection coefficients), conduction through the rotor body, and radiation at elevated temperatures. The transient solver captured the rapid heat spike during braking and the cooling curve post-stop.

Design Optimization

Thermal hotspots concentrated at the friction interface, particularly toward the outer radius. Two geometric modifications were evaluated: drilled ventilation holes and internal radial vanes. Both increased surface area and promoted airflow. The vaned design showed superior thermal distribution without compromising structural integrity under bending and torsion loads.

Result

Optimized rotor geometry achieved approximately 8% reduction in peak interface temperature versus the baseline design. The optimization was achieved without increasing rotor mass or sacrificing structural margins — demonstrating simulation-driven design as a practical engineering tool.

Embedded Systems · Robotics

Arduino Obstacle-Avoiding Rover

Built an autonomous ground rover with real-time obstacle detection and dynamic path rerouting using ultrasonic sensing.

System Architecture

Platform: Arduino Uno controlling a 4-wheel drive chassis. Sensing is handled by an HC-SR04 ultrasonic module mounted on an SG90 servo — the servo sweeps left and right to scan the field ahead, giving the rover directional obstacle awareness rather than just a binary detect/no-detect signal.

Detection Logic

Ultrasonic pulses are fired at regular intervals during forward travel. When an obstacle is detected within ~6 inches (~15 cm), the rover halts, sweeps the sensor, and evaluates clearance distance on both flanks. The path with greater clearance is selected and a turn of proportional angle is executed before resuming forward travel.

Mechanical Integration

The servo mount was designed and fabricated to hold the ultrasonic sensor at the optimal height and angle for ground-level obstacle detection. Motor drive logic was tuned to account for real-world drivetrain asymmetry — calibrating left-right motor speeds to achieve straight-line tracking on the forward path.

Key Takeaway

The project demonstrated that autonomous behavior doesn't require complex hardware — it requires precise logic, reliable sensing, and a well-calibrated mechanical platform. The integration of embedded software with physical systems is where the real engineering challenge lives.
